## Career Vision

Help the user define a multi-horizon career vision tied to their stated goal.

- **1-Year Vision**: What does success look like in 12 months? What title, responsibilities, or outcomes would signal you are on track?
- **3-Year Vision**: Where do you want to be positioned in your field? What kind of work do you want to own? What reputation do you want to have built?
- **5-Year Vision**: What is the larger ambition — leadership, domain expertise, entrepreneurship, financial independence, impact?

Questions to ask yourself:
- If I had no fear of failure, what would I be doing in 5 years?
- What kind of problems do I want to spend my days solving?
- Who do I admire in this field, and what specifically do I want to emulate?
- What does "winning" look like for me — and is it about title, money, autonomy, impact, or something else?

Encourage the user to write down each vision in 2–3 sentences, present tense, as if it has already happened.

## Current State Assessment

Help the user honestly audit where they are today before planning where to go.

### Skills Inventory
- List the core technical and soft skills required for the target role or goal
- Rate current proficiency in each: Beginner / Developing / Proficient / Expert
- Identify the top 3 skills that are already strengths and the top 3 that need the most work

### Strengths and Weaknesses
- **Strengths**: What do people consistently come to you for? What do you do faster or better than your peers?
- **Weaknesses**: What do you avoid? Where do you feel least confident? What feedback keeps recurring in performance reviews?

### What You Are Known For vs. What You Want to Be Known For
- Write one sentence describing what you are currently known for at work or in your industry
- Write one sentence describing what you want to be known for in 3 years
- Identify the gap between these two statements — this is your personal brand delta

## Gap Analysis

Identify the specific delta between your current state and your target goal across four dimensions.

### Skills Gap
- List skills the target role requires that you do not yet have at the required level
- For each gap, estimate the time and effort needed to close it (weeks, months, years)
- Prioritize by which gaps are blockers vs. nice-to-haves

### Experience Gap
- What kinds of projects, scope, or responsibilities does the target role require that you have not yet had?
- Where can you get that experience — stretch assignments, side projects, open source, freelance, internal transfers?

### Network Gap
- Who are the key people (mentors, sponsors, peers, hiring managers) you need to know to reach your goal?
- Where are those people found — conferences, communities, LinkedIn, alumni networks?
- How many meaningful professional relationships do you currently have in the target domain vs. how many you need?

### Credentials Gap
- Does the target role require certifications, degrees, portfolios, or publications you do not yet have?
- Evaluate which credentials are actually required vs. which are optional signals
- Identify the fastest credible path to any required credentials

## Goal Setting Framework

Translate the vision and gap analysis into concrete, achievable goals.

### SMART Goals for Career
Apply each SMART criterion to career goals:
- **Specific**: "Get promoted to Senior Engineer" not "advance my career"
- **Measurable**: Define the exact metric — a title change, a comp number, a portfolio piece, a certification
- **Achievable**: Stretch but realistic — ambitious enough to require effort, realistic enough to maintain momentum
- **Relevant**: Every goal should directly close one of the gaps identified above
- **Time-bound**: Assign a clear deadline — quarterly, annual, or 18-month goals work well

Example SMART goal structure for the stated role/goal — generate 2–3 example goals tailored to $ARGUMENTS.

### OKRs for Personal Use
Adapt the OKR (Objectives and Key Results) framework for individual career use:
- **Objective**: A qualitative, inspiring statement of where you want to go (e.g., "Become a recognized expert in [domain]")
- **Key Results** (3–5 per objective): Quantitative, binary outcomes that prove you hit the objective
  - Example: "Publish 4 technical articles," "Speak at 1 conference," "Complete [certification]"
- Set OKRs quarterly; review monthly; be honest about red/yellow/green status

### Prioritization When Everything Feels Urgent
- Use the **Impact vs. Effort matrix**: plot each goal on a 2x2 grid — high impact + low effort goals go first
- Ask: "Which one goal, if achieved, would make all others easier or unnecessary?" — do that one first
- Protect at least 20% of your time for strategic career development, not just day-to-day work deliverables
- Say no to opportunities that do not move you toward your 1-year or 3-year vision, even if they feel flattering

## Growth Roadmap

Turn goals into a concrete action plan with built-in review points.

### 90-Day Action Plan
Structure the first 90 days into three phases:

- **Days 1–30 (Foundation)**: Close the most critical knowledge gaps; establish learning habits; identify 2–3 key people to connect with
- **Days 31–60 (Momentum)**: Begin producing visible work — a project, article, contribution, or result that demonstrates progress; get feedback early
- **Days 61–90 (Acceleration)**: Review what is working; double down on high-leverage activities; set next quarter's OKRs

For $ARGUMENTS, suggest 3–5 specific actions for each phase.

### Quarterly Check-ins
- Schedule a 60-minute solo review every 90 days
- Review: What did I accomplish? What did I learn? What should I stop, start, or continue?
- Update your skills inventory and gap analysis — gaps that were closed should be celebrated and replaced with new ones
- Adjust timelines honestly — missing a deadline is data, not failure

### When to Pivot vs. Stay the Course
- **Pivot signals**: You have consistently missed goals for 2+ quarters, the market for the role has shifted, or your values no longer align with the direction
- **Stay the course signals**: Progress is slower than expected but still directionally correct; feedback is positive even if milestones are delayed; setbacks are situational, not structural
- A pivot is not giving up — it is updating your strategy with new information. Revisit the vision, not just the tactics.

## Career Tracking

What gets measured gets managed. Track these metrics consistently.

### Output Metrics
- Projects shipped or completed (quantity and scope)
- Skills added or leveled up (track in a simple spreadsheet or Notion page)
- Courses, certifications, or books completed per quarter

### Outcome Metrics
- Promotions or title changes (and the timeline between them)
- Compensation growth (year-over-year percentage; compare to market data)
- Scope of responsibility (team size, budget, revenue, or users impacted)

### Network Metrics
- New meaningful professional connections added per quarter
- Number of people who have referred you, vouched for you, or opened a door
- Mentors and sponsors — do you have at least one of each?

### Visibility Metrics
- Public artifacts: articles written, talks given, open source contributions, media appearances
- Internal visibility: presentations given, cross-functional projects led, recognition received
- Inbound opportunities (recruiters reaching out, speaking invitations, consulting requests) — a leading indicator that your brand is working

### Recommended Tracking Cadence
- **Weekly**: Log key wins, blockers, and one thing learned
- **Monthly**: Review OKR key results; update skills inventory
- **Quarterly**: Full career review; refresh the 90-day plan; assess gap closure
- **Annually**: Revisit the 1-year and 3-year vision; recalibrate compensation benchmarks; update your resume and LinkedIn even if not job searching
