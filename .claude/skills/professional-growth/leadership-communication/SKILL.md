## Executive Presence

**What it is:**
- Executive presence is the combination of confidence, credibility, and clarity that makes others trust your judgment and want to follow your lead. It is not about personality type — it is a learnable set of behaviors.

**How to develop it:**
- Know your material cold before any high-stakes conversation or meeting. Preparation is the foundation of confidence.
- Slow down when you speak. Rushed speech signals anxiety; measured speech signals control.
- Eliminate filler words (um, uh, like, you know). Record yourself to identify your patterns, then practice pausing instead of filling.
- Adopt a composed physical presence: steady eye contact, open posture, deliberate gestures.
- Give your opinions, not just options. Leaders are expected to have a point of view.

**Speaking with confidence:**
- Lead with the conclusion, then support it. Do not build up to your point — state it first.
- Use "I think" and "I recommend" rather than "maybe" or "it might be worth considering."
- Disagree constructively and on record when you have a real objection. Staying silent to avoid conflict erodes trust over time.

**Commanding a room:**
- Arrive early and greet people before the meeting begins. Relationships shape who listens to whom.
- Speak to the whole room, not just the person who asked the question.
- When interrupted, finish your point calmly: "Let me finish this thought and then I'd love to hear yours."

**The importance of conciseness:**
- Every extra word dilutes your message. Edit ruthlessly before you speak or send.
- The "one breath" test: if you can't summarize your point in one breath, you don't know it well enough yet.
- Respect other people's time as a form of credibility-building.

## Influence Without Authority

**Getting buy-in from peers and stakeholders:**
- Influence is earned, not assigned. Start by understanding what the other person cares about before pitching anything.
- Ask before telling: "What's your biggest concern with this approach?" surfaces objections early and shows you're listening.
- Frame your ideas in terms of their goals, not yours. "This will help your team ship faster" lands better than "this is how I want to do it."
- Build relationships when you don't need anything. Influence accrues over time; you can't manufacture it in the moment you need it.

**Persuasion frameworks:**

*SCQA (Situation, Complication, Question, Answer):*
- **Situation:** Establish shared context — what is true today that everyone agrees on.
- **Complication:** Introduce the tension — what has changed, what problem exists, what is at risk.
- **Question:** Make the so-what explicit — what does this mean we need to figure out?
- **Answer:** Deliver your recommendation clearly and directly.

*Pyramid Principle:*
- Lead with your governing thought (the main recommendation or conclusion).
- Support it with 3-5 key arguments or findings.
- Back each argument with data, examples, or evidence.
- Use this structure for documents, presentations, and long-form emails.

**Reading the room:**
- Before speaking, scan: who is nodding, who looks skeptical, who is disengaged?
- Adjust in real time — if a key stakeholder looks confused, pause and check in rather than pushing forward.
- Identify the informal decision-maker in the room; they may not be the highest-ranking person present.
- After a meeting, follow up with the skeptic privately. Public disagreements rarely change minds.

## Written Communication

**Email etiquette for senior audiences:**
- Put the ask or recommendation in the first sentence. Senior readers skim; make it easy for them.
- Use the subject line as a headline: "Decision needed: Q3 roadmap prioritization" beats "Q3 update."
- Keep it to five sentences or fewer when possible. If it needs to be long, use headers and bullets.
- State what you need from the reader explicitly: "I need your approval by Friday" or "No action needed — FYI only."
- Never send an email when you are emotional. Draft it, wait an hour, then decide if it still needs to be sent.

**Writing crisp Slack messages:**
- Front-load the context and ask: "Hey [name] — need your input on X by EOD. Can you review this doc? [link]"
- Avoid drive-by pings ("Hey, do you have a sec?") without context. Give people enough to prepare before they respond.
- Use threads to keep channels readable. Respond in thread, not in channel, unless the update is relevant to everyone.
- Long explanations belong in a doc, not a Slack message. Link, don't paste.

**Structuring documents and proposals:**
- Every document should answer: what is the situation, what is the recommendation, and what do you need from the reader?
- Use an executive summary at the top — one paragraph, the whole story.
- Use headers so readers can navigate without reading every word.
- Appendices are your friend. Put supporting detail there so the main document stays clean.
- Proofread for clarity, not just grammar. Read it out loud — if you stumble, the sentence needs rewriting.

## Giving & Receiving Feedback

**The SBI Feedback Model (Situation, Behavior, Impact):**
- **Situation:** Anchor the feedback to a specific moment. "In yesterday's all-hands..." — not "you always..."
- **Behavior:** Describe what you observed, not what you interpreted or assumed. "You talked over three people who tried to contribute" — not "you were being aggressive."
- **Impact:** Explain the effect it had. "It made it harder for the team to feel safe sharing ideas, and we may have missed important input."
- Follow with a question or request: "I'd love to understand what was going on for you" or "Going forward, could you try...?"

**Delivering hard feedback:**
- Do it promptly. Delayed feedback loses context and sends the message that the behavior was acceptable.
- Do it privately. Public feedback is humiliating, not developmental.
- Be specific and direct. Vague feedback ("just be more collaborative") is useless. Name the behavior, name the impact.
- Separate the person from the behavior. "This approach isn't working" not "you aren't working."
- Check for understanding: "Does this land? What's your reaction?"

**Receiving feedback without getting defensive:**
- Your first job is to listen, not to respond. Let the feedback land before you say anything.
- Resist the urge to explain or justify immediately. It signals you're not really taking it in.
- Ask clarifying questions: "Can you give me a specific example?" or "What would you have wanted to see instead?"
- Say thank you — even if you disagree. Feedback is a gift of someone's time and attention.
- Decide what to do with it later. Not all feedback is correct, but all of it is worth considering.

## Managing Up

**Building trust with your manager:**
- Deliver on your commitments. Trust is built through reliability, not through impressive presentations.
- Surface problems early, with a proposed solution or at least a clear picture of the options. Never let your manager be blindsided.
- Be explicit about what you need: "I'm looking for a decision, not advice" or "I just need to think out loud — you don't need to solve this."
- Understand their pressures. What are they being measured on? What keeps them up at night? Align your work to what matters to them.

**Keeping them informed without over-communicating:**
- Agree on a cadence and format upfront. "Would a weekly bullet summary work, or do you prefer I flag things in real time?"
- Share outcomes and blockers, not a diary of your activities.
- Use the "no surprises" rule: anything that could become a problem should be flagged proactively, before it is a problem.
- If something changes in a plan you've already aligned on, tell them before they hear it from someone else.

**Asking for what you need:**
- Be direct: "I'd like to be considered for X" or "I need clearer direction on Y to move forward."
- Bring data when asking for resources, headcount, or scope: "Here's what we could ship with one more engineer, and here's what we can't."
- Timing matters. Ask when your manager is not in crisis mode. Ask when you've recently delivered something well.
- If you're not getting what you need, name it: "I feel like I'm not getting enough visibility into the decisions that affect my work. Can we fix that?"

## Managing & Mentoring Others

**1:1 best practices:**
- The 1:1 is their meeting, not yours. Come with an agenda, but let them drive it.
- Ask better questions: "What's blocking you?" "What are you most uncertain about?" "What do you wish I understood about your situation?"
- Don't make it a status update. Status belongs in async channels. Use 1:1 time for growth, blockers, and relationship.
- Follow through on what you say you'll do. Nothing erodes trust faster than a manager who doesn't keep their commitments.
- Check in on the whole person, not just the work. Energy, morale, and life circumstances all affect performance.

**Delegation without micromanaging:**
- Delegate outcomes, not tasks. "I need a plan for onboarding new partners by end of month" — not a step-by-step checklist.
- Be clear about constraints: timeline, budget, who needs to be consulted, what decisions they can make without you.
- Define checkpoints upfront, so you're not checking in out of anxiety. "Let's sync at the halfway point and before you finalize."
- Resist the urge to redo their work because it is not how you would have done it. Different is not wrong.
- Give credit publicly and specifically. "This was [name]'s design — they did great work here."

**Developing people around you even if you're not a formal manager:**
- Sponsor people, don't just mentor them. Mentoring is advice; sponsorship is putting your reputation behind someone in a room they're not in.
- Share context that others might not have access to. Information is a form of development.
- Create visibility opportunities: invite junior colleagues to present their own work, bring them into key meetings, introduce them to senior stakeholders.
- Give real feedback, not just praise. People cannot grow if they only hear what they're doing well.
- Model the behaviors you want to see. How you show up — your intellectual honesty, your care, your standards — teaches more than any piece of advice.
