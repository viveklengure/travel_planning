## Resume & LinkedIn Optimization Guide

Use the sections below to build a compelling professional presence tailored to your target role or goal. Every recommendation is actionable — work through each section in order.

---

## Resume Structure

**Choose the Right Format**

- **Chronological** — Best for candidates with a consistent, upward career trajectory in the same field. Lists experience newest-first. Preferred by most recruiters and ATS systems.
- **Functional** — Emphasizes skills over timeline. Use only if you have significant gaps or are making a drastic career pivot. ATS systems often struggle with this format — use cautiously.
- **Hybrid (Combination)** — Opens with a skills summary or core competencies section, then lists chronological experience. Best for career changers, returning professionals, or senior candidates with broad skill sets.

**Length Rules**

- 0–10 years of experience: 1 page, no exceptions.
- 10+ years: 2 pages maximum. Only extend to 2 pages if every line adds value.
- Executive/academic/federal resumes may go longer — these are exceptions, not norms.

**What to Include**

- Contact info (name, city/state, phone, professional email, LinkedIn URL, portfolio/GitHub if relevant)
- Professional summary (2–3 lines max — who you are, your specialty, your value proposition)
- Core competencies or skills section (6–12 keywords in a scannable grid)
- Work experience (company, title, dates, location, 3–5 bullets per role)
- Education (degree, institution, graduation year — omit GPA unless above 3.5 and within 3 years of graduation)
- Certifications, licenses, or publications (if relevant to the role)

**What to Exclude**

- Objective statements (replace with a professional summary)
- References or "References available upon request"
- Photos, headshots, or personal details (age, marital status, nationality)
- Every job you have ever held — cut anything older than 15 years unless it is uniquely relevant
- Soft skills listed in isolation ("team player," "hard worker") — these belong inside bullets with evidence
- Full mailing address — city and state are sufficient

## Writing Impactful Bullets

**The XYZ Formula (Google's Recommended Approach)**

> Accomplished **[X]** by doing **[Y]**, resulting in **[Z]**.

Every bullet should answer: What did you do, how did you do it, and what happened as a result?

**Examples**

- Weak: "Responsible for managing social media accounts."
- Strong: "Grew Instagram following by 47% in 6 months by launching a weekly video series, driving a 22% increase in inbound leads."

- Weak: "Helped improve customer satisfaction scores."
- Strong: "Raised NPS from 61 to 78 over two quarters by redesigning the onboarding flow and introducing proactive check-in calls for new accounts."

**Quantifying Achievements — What to Measure**

- Percentages: growth, reduction, improvement rates
- Dollar amounts: revenue generated, costs cut, budget managed
- Volume: number of clients, transactions, users, hires, SKUs
- Time: hours saved per week, project delivered X days ahead of schedule
- Scale: team size managed, markets entered, countries served

If you cannot find an exact number, use a reasonable range or qualifier ("approximately," "up to," "more than").

**Action Verb List by Category**

- Leadership: Directed, Spearheaded, Championed, Oversaw, Mentored, Mobilized
- Growth/Sales: Generated, Drove, Expanded, Captured, Negotiated, Secured
- Analysis: Analyzed, Evaluated, Benchmarked, Forecasted, Audited, Mapped
- Building/Creating: Developed, Designed, Engineered, Launched, Established, Architected
- Improving: Streamlined, Optimized, Restructured, Reduced, Automated, Revamped
- Collaboration: Partnered, Coordinated, Liaised, Facilitated, Unified, Aligned

Never start two consecutive bullets with the same verb.

## Tailoring for the Role

**Mirror Job Description Language**

- Copy the exact phrases the job posting uses for key requirements. If the JD says "cross-functional stakeholder management," your resume should say that — not "working with different teams."
- Identify the 5–8 most repeated or emphasized skills in the posting and ensure each appears at least once on your resume, in context.
- Reorder your core competencies section to front-load keywords matching each specific application.

**ATS Optimization**

- Use standard section headers: "Work Experience," "Education," "Skills" — not creative alternatives like "Where I Have Been" or "My Toolkit."
- Avoid tables, text boxes, columns, headers/footers, and graphics. ATS parsers often skip or scramble these.
- Save and submit as a .docx or plain PDF (not a "designed" PDF exported from Canva or similar tools unless explicitly allowed).
- Spell out acronyms at least once: "Search Engine Optimization (SEO)."
- Do not use the same keyword so many times it reads as keyword stuffing — once or twice per section is enough.

**Keyword Placement Priority**

1. Professional summary (highest weight in many ATS systems)
2. Core competencies / skills section
3. Job title lines
4. Bullet points in most recent 2 roles
5. Education and certifications

## LinkedIn Profile Optimization

**Headline Formula**

Your headline is the single most important piece of real estate on LinkedIn. It appears in search results, connection requests, and comment threads.

Formula:
> [Current Role or Target Role] | [Specialty or Differentiator] | [Who You Help or Key Result]

Examples:
- "Senior Product Manager | B2B SaaS | Turning customer insights into 0-to-1 products"
- "Data Engineer | Streaming Pipelines & Cloud Infrastructure | Building systems that scale"
- "Career Changer | Ex-Teacher Transitioning into UX Research | Human-centered problem solver"

Do not just list your job title and company. That is the default — and the default is forgettable.

**Summary / About Section**

- Length: 3–5 short paragraphs or a mix of paragraphs and bullets. You have 2,600 characters — use at least 1,200.
- Open with a hook — a specific achievement, a belief you hold, or a problem you solve. Recruiters decide in 3 seconds whether to keep reading.
- Write in first person ("I build," not "John builds"). LinkedIn is a human platform.
- Include: your specialty, industries you have worked in, what you are known for, what you are looking for or open to.
- End with a call to action: "Open to roles in X" or "Feel free to connect if you are working on Y."
- Seed your summary with 5–8 keywords naturally — this directly affects LinkedIn search ranking.

**Experience Section vs. Your Resume**

- LinkedIn experience can be slightly more narrative and less tightly formatted than your resume.
- You can include more context, links, media, and projects.
- Add media attachments to experience entries: slide decks, case studies, articles, project screenshots.
- Keep bullet content consistent with your resume in substance, but LinkedIn allows fuller storytelling.

**Skills and Endorsements**

- Add up to 50 skills. Prioritize the top 10 carefully — these appear above the fold and carry the most weight.
- Pin your 3 most important skills in the "Top Skills" spots.
- Ask 3–5 colleagues to endorse your top skills; endorsements from people with relevant titles carry more weight.
- Reciprocate endorsements to maintain relationships.

**Featured Section**

- Use this section — most people do not, which makes it an easy differentiator.
- Pin 2–4 items: a published article, a portfolio link, a case study, a LinkedIn post that performed well, a presentation, or an award announcement.
- Order them by relevance to your target role, not by date.

## LinkedIn Activity Strategy

**Posting Frequency**

- Minimum for credibility: 1 post per week.
- Optimal for growth: 3–5 posts per week.
- Consistency beats frequency — a reliable weekly post outperforms a burst of daily posts followed by silence.

**Content That Builds Credibility**

- Lessons learned from a project or mistake (authentic and highly shareable)
- A result you achieved and the specific approach that drove it
- Your take on an industry trend or news item (add your analysis — do not just reshare)
- A resource you found useful, with a sentence explaining why
- Career milestone posts (new role, promotion, certification) — do these sparingly, 2–3 per year

**Content to Avoid**

- Vague motivational quotes with no professional context
- Posting about personal life unless it connects directly to a professional insight
- Engagement-bait ("Comment YES if you agree") — LinkedIn's algorithm has penalized this
- Reposting without adding your own commentary

**Engaging Without Being Spammy**

- Comment on 5–10 posts per week from people in your target industry. Write substantive comments (2+ sentences) — not just "Great post!"
- Follow target company pages and engage with their content before applying.
- Send connection requests with a short, personalized note (1–2 sentences explaining why you are connecting). Never use the default "I'd like to connect."
- Message hiring managers or employees at target companies before or after applying — keep it brief, genuine, and focused on them, not your needs.

## Common Mistakes

**Resume Mistakes**

| Mistake | Why It Gets You Filtered | Fix |
|---|---|---|
| Generic objective statement | Signals you copy-pasted your resume | Replace with a 2–3 line professional summary tailored to the role |
| Duties-focused bullets | Tells recruiters what your job was, not what you did | Rewrite every bullet with the XYZ formula and at least one metric |
| Inconsistent formatting | Signals low attention to detail | Use a single font, consistent date format, and uniform bullet style throughout |
| Including irrelevant jobs | Wastes limited resume space | Cut roles older than 15 years or unrelated to your target unless they add unique value |
| Using a resume email like hotmail or aol | Creates an outdated impression | Use a Gmail address formatted as firstname.lastname |
| One resume for all applications | Fails ATS keyword matching | Maintain a master resume and create tailored versions for each application |
| No summary or skills section | Misses ATS keyword opportunities | Add both — these sections are heavily weighted by ATS parsers |

**LinkedIn Mistakes**

| Mistake | Why It Hurts You | Fix |
|---|---|---|
| Default headline (just job title + company) | Invisible in search results | Write a keyword-rich headline using the formula above |
| Blank or thin About section | Missed opportunity to tell your story and rank in search | Write at least 1,200 characters with a hook, your specialty, and a CTA |
| No profile photo | Profiles without photos receive up to 21x fewer views | Use a professional, well-lit headshot with a clean background |
| Open to Work banner visible to all | Can signal desperation to some recruiters | Set "Open to Work" to visible only to recruiters, not your full network |
| Never posting or engaging | LinkedIn's algorithm buries inactive profiles in search | Post once per week minimum and comment on others' content regularly |
| Sending mass connection requests with no note | Low acceptance rate, can get your account flagged | Always include a short, personalized message |
| Endorsing people you do not know | Devalues endorsements as a signal | Only endorse skills you have genuinely witnessed |

*Build your resume and LinkedIn profile as a system, not two separate documents. They should tell the same story with the same keywords — one is the formal artifact, the other is the living, searchable, social proof.*
