## Networking Mindset

A great network is built on genuine relationships, not a list of contacts.

- **Give before you get.** Lead every interaction by asking what you can offer — a useful article, an introduction, a piece of feedback, a kind word. People remember who helped them.
- **Quality over quantity.** Ten people who truly know your work and would vouch for you are worth more than 500 LinkedIn connections who can't recall your name.
- **Think long-term.** The best networking investment pays off years from now. Plant seeds without expecting immediate harvest.
- **Reframe the goal.** You're not "networking" — you're meeting interesting people in your field. Shift from transactional to curious, and conversations become natural.
- **Be consistent, not opportunistic.** Reaching out only when you need something erodes trust fast. Build relationships before you need them.

## Mapping Your Network

Before reaching out to new people, audit what you already have.

### Strong Ties vs. Weak Ties
- **Strong ties** — close colleagues, former managers, trusted friends in your field. High trust, but limited new information (you already know what they know).
- **Weak ties** — acquaintances, former classmates, people you've met once. Lower trust, but they open doors to entirely different worlds. Research shows weak ties drive most job opportunities and idea flow.

### How to Audit Your Network
1. Open LinkedIn (or your contacts) and scroll through everyone you know professionally.
2. Tag each person by: **industry**, **seniority level** (peer / mid / senior / executive), and **function** (engineering, marketing, ops, finance, etc.).
3. Identify where you are under-represented:
   - Are you missing senior people who can sponsor or advocate for you?
   - Are you missing people outside your current industry (if you want to pivot)?
   - Are you missing peers at companies you admire?
   - Are you missing people in functions adjacent to yours?

### Identifying Gaps
- Write down your 6–12 month career goal.
- List 3–5 types of people who could directly help you reach it (e.g., hiring managers at target companies, founders in your target space, domain experts).
- These gaps become your outreach priorities.

## Outreach Strategy

Cold outreach works when it's specific, short, and genuinely about the other person.

### Cold Outreach Templates That Work

**LinkedIn connection request (300 character limit — keep it tight):**
> "Hi [Name] — I've been following your work on [specific topic/company]. I'm [one line about you] and would love to connect. No ask — just think we'd have a lot to learn from each other."

**Email or LinkedIn message for a coffee chat:**
> Subject: Quick intro — [your name], [your role]
>
> Hi [Name],
>
> I came across your work on [specific thing] and really appreciated [specific detail]. I'm currently [brief context about yourself and what you're working on].
>
> I'd love to hear your perspective on [specific topic relevant to them]. Would you be open to a 20-minute call in the next few weeks? Happy to work around your schedule.
>
> Either way, thanks for the work you put out — it's been genuinely useful.
>
> [Your name]

### What Works
- Mention something **specific** they've written, said, or built — it proves you did your homework.
- Keep it **short**. Three paragraphs max.
- Make the ask **small and clear** — a 20-minute call, not a mentorship or a job referral.
- Give them an **easy out** ("no worries if you're swamped").

### What to Avoid
- "I'd love to pick your brain" — vague and one-sided.
- Long intros about yourself before making any connection to them.
- Asking for a job, referral, or intro in the first message.
- Copy-paste messages that clearly haven't been personalized.

### Follow-Up Cadence
- Send a first message.
- If no reply after **7–10 days**, send one polite follow-up: "Just wanted to bump this up in case it got buried — no pressure at all."
- If still no reply, move on. One follow-up is professional; two or more becomes pressure.
- After a call: send a thank-you **within 24 hours**. Include one specific thing you appreciated, and any resource or intro you promised.

## In-Person and Virtual Events

Events are where serendipity happens — but only if you work them intentionally.

### Before the Event
- Review the attendee list or speaker lineup in advance.
- Identify 3–5 people you'd genuinely love to meet and why.
- Prepare 2–3 conversation starters relevant to the event theme.

### How to Work a Room
- Arrive early. The room is less crowded, people are more open, and you can have real conversations before the noise ramps up.
- Position yourself near natural gathering points: the registration table, the coffee station, the edge of a circle of people.
- Start conversations with context: "What brought you to this event?" or "Are you a speaker or attendee?"
- Listen more than you talk. Ask follow-up questions. People remember how you made them feel.
- Gracefully exit conversations: "I want to let you mingle — it was great meeting you. Can I connect on LinkedIn?"

### Conversation Starters That Never Get Old
- "What's the most interesting thing you're working on right now?"
- "What made you decide to come to this one specifically?"
- "What's something you wish more people in [industry] were talking about?"
- "How did you end up in [their field / role]?"

### Virtual Events
- Turn your camera on and use a clean, well-lit background — it signals engagement.
- Use the chat actively: comment on talks, ask questions, introduce yourself.
- In breakout rooms, take initiative to introduce yourself first — it sets a warm tone.
- DM interesting people during or immediately after the event while context is fresh.

### Following Up After Events (Within 24 Hours)
- Send a message referencing something specific from your conversation: "Loved what you said about [topic] — made me think about [connection to your work]."
- Include your LinkedIn if you exchanged cards; include a relevant article or resource if relevant.
- Suggest a next step if appropriate: "Would love to continue the conversation — up for a quick call sometime?"

## Building a Personal Board of Advisors

Think of your career like a company — you need a board to help you navigate it well.

### What It Is
A small, intentional group of 4–6 people who know you, believe in you, and give you honest guidance. Not a formal arrangement — just relationships you invest in deliberately.

### Who to Include

| Role | What They Offer |
|------|----------------|
| **Mentor** | Someone 5–10 years ahead of you in your field. Wisdom, pattern recognition, mistakes to avoid. |
| **Sponsor** | Someone with organizational power who actively advocates for you in rooms you're not in. |
| **Peer** | Someone at your level who is also growing fast. Shared language, mutual accountability, co-learning. |
| **Industry Expert** | Deep domain knowledge in an area you want to grow in. Keeps you sharp on trends and credibility. |
| **Wildcard / Cross-Industry** | Someone from a completely different field. Helps you see blind spots and bring fresh thinking. |

### How to Build These Relationships
- Don't ask someone to be your mentor in the first message. Build the relationship first through a few conversations.
- Make it easy and low-pressure: start with a single call, then stay in touch consistently.
- Show up prepared, take notes, follow through on anything you said you'd do.
- Send updates — let people see you acting on their advice. It makes them invested in your success.

### How to Maintain Them
- Aim for a touchpoint every 1–3 months depending on the relationship.
- Come with something to share or ask, not just an open-ended "what's new."
- Express gratitude specifically: not just "thanks for the chat" but "the thing you said about X changed how I'm approaching Y."

## Maintaining Connections

The real skill in networking isn't meeting people — it's staying connected over time.

### Staying Top of Mind (Without Being Annoying)
The goal is to be a **value-add presence**, not a recurring ask. Touch base when you have something worth sharing:
- A relevant article or podcast you thought of because of a past conversation.
- A congratulations on a promotion, funding round, new role, or published piece.
- An introduction to someone they'd genuinely benefit from knowing.
- A quick check-in with context: "Saw [X] in the news and thought of our conversation about [Y]."

### A Simple CRM for Your Network
You don't need fancy software. A spreadsheet or Notion table works well.

**Columns to track:**
- Name, company, role
- How you met
- Last touchpoint date
- Next planned touchpoint
- Notes (what they care about, life updates, things you want to follow up on)

**Review it monthly.** Flag anyone you haven't spoken to in 3+ months who matters to your current goals. Reach out.

### Touchpoint Ideas
- Share an article with a one-line personal note: "Thought of you because of X."
- Congratulate them on a public win (LinkedIn promotion, company milestone, publication).
- Make an introduction: "I think you and [Name] should know each other — here's why." (Always ask both sides first.)
- Comment thoughtfully on their LinkedIn posts or writing.
- Invite them to something relevant: an event, a webinar, a small gathering.
- Send a "how are things going with [project they mentioned]?" message — shows you were listening.

### The Golden Rule of Maintenance
Never reach out only when you need something. If the only time someone hears from you is when you're job searching or fundraising, they'll notice. Invest consistently — even a 5-minute message every few months keeps a relationship alive.
