## Know Your Market Value

**Why this matters:** You cannot negotiate effectively without a number anchored in data. Gut feelings and "what I made last time" are not strategies.

**Where to research:**

- **Levels.fyi** — Best for tech roles (software engineers, PMs, designers). Shows total comp breakdowns by company, level, and location. Filter by YOE (years of experience) and role level.
- **Glassdoor** — Broad industry coverage. Salary reports are self-reported; cross-reference with at least one other source.
- **LinkedIn Salary** — Useful for filtering by geography, industry, and seniority. Requires premium or a free peek.
- **Blind** — Anonymous posts from verified employees. Good for real-talk numbers and equity details at specific companies.
- **Recruiter conversations** — Recruiters talk to hundreds of candidates. Ask directly: "What's the budgeted range for this role?" Many will tell you, especially at the offer stage.
- **Offer letters from competing companies** — The single most powerful data point. Even a competing offer you're not excited about raises your floor.

**How to triangulate a target number:**

1. Gather at least 3–5 data points from the sources above.
2. Identify the median and the 75th percentile for your target role + location + level.
3. Set your **target** at the 75th percentile — this is what you ask for.
4. Set your **floor** at the median — this is the minimum you'd accept without serious hesitation.
5. Never anchor below your floor. Walking away is always an option.

## Total Compensation Breakdown

Salary is one line item. Total compensation (TC) is the full picture. Always evaluate and negotiate the whole package.

**Base Salary**
- The foundation. Affects bonuses (often a % of base), 401k matches, and future raises (which compound on top of base).
- Easiest to compare across companies. Push hard here first.

**Annual Bonus**
- Usually expressed as a % of base (e.g., 10–20% target bonus).
- Ask: Is it guaranteed or performance-dependent? What's the historical payout rate? Is there a cliff (no payout if you leave before a date)?

**Equity**
- **RSUs (Restricted Stock Units):** You receive shares that vest over time (commonly 4 years with a 1-year cliff). Value is tied to current stock price. Lower risk than options.
- **Stock Options:** You get the right to *buy* shares at a set price (strike price). Only valuable if the stock price rises above the strike price. Common at startups.
- Ask: What's the vesting schedule? Is there acceleration on acquisition? What's the current 409A valuation (for options at private companies)?
- To value equity: `(# of shares x current price or 409A valuation) / vesting years = annual equity value`

**Benefits**
- Health, dental, vision — compare plan quality and employee premium costs.
- 401k match — A 4% match on a $150k salary is $6,000/year. This is real money.
- HSA/FSA contributions, life insurance, disability coverage.

**PTO & Flexibility**
- Unlimited PTO sounds good but often results in *less* time taken. Ask: What's the average PTO taken by employees?
- Remote work flexibility has real financial value (no commute, relocation avoided, housing cost arbitrage).
- Signing bonuses, relocation packages, and education stipends are also negotiable.

**How to compare packages:**
Build a simple spreadsheet. For each offer or benchmark: `base + expected bonus + annual equity value + 401k match + estimated benefits value = true annual TC`.

## Timing

**The golden rule:** Never name a number first, and never negotiate salary before you have an offer in hand.

**Before the offer — handling "What are your salary expectations?"**

This question, asked during screening or early interviews, is a trap. The goal is to get you to anchor low or disqualify yourself. Deflect with confidence:

- *"I'm still learning about the full scope of the role. I'd prefer to wait until I understand the full picture before discussing compensation — I'm confident we can find something that works for both of us."*
- *"I'm open to a competitive offer based on the role's responsibilities and the market. What's the budgeted range for this position?"*
- If pressed hard: Give a wide range anchored at your target, e.g., *"Based on my research, I'm targeting the $X–$Y range for total compensation, but I'm flexible depending on the full package."*

**After the offer:**

- You have maximum leverage at this moment. The company has invested time and wants you.
- Never accept on the spot. Say: *"Thank you — I'm really excited about this opportunity. Can I have a few days to review the offer carefully?"*
- Review the full package, compare to your research, and prepare your counter.

## Negotiation Scripts

**Countering an initial offer (base salary):**

> "Thank you so much for the offer — I'm genuinely excited about this role and the team. After reviewing the details and doing some research on market rates for this level in [location/industry], I was expecting something closer to $[target]. Is there flexibility to get to that number?"

Keep it short. State your number. Stop talking. Let them respond.

**Asking for more equity when base is capped:**

> "I understand there may be constraints on base. Would it be possible to make up some of that gap with additional equity? Even an extra [X shares / $X in RSUs] over the vesting period would help bridge the difference."

**Negotiating a raise with your current employer:**

> "I'd like to talk about my compensation. Over the past [time period], I've [specific impact: led X, delivered Y, grew Z by %]. Based on my research into market rates for someone at my level doing this type of work, and the value I've been delivering, I believe a salary of $[target] is appropriate. I want to stay and keep growing here — can we make that happen?"

**Handling a low offer with a competing offer:**

> "I want to be transparent with you — I do have another offer at $[number] in total comp. This role is actually my first choice, and I'd prefer to be here. But I need to make a financially sound decision. If you're able to get to $[your target], I'm ready to sign."

## Handling Pushback

**"This is our best offer."**

This phrase is almost never literally true. It means: *this is our best offer without a compelling reason to do better.*

Your response:
> "I appreciate that — I know compensation decisions involve a lot of moving parts. Is there any flexibility at all, even on the equity or signing bonus side? I really want to make this work."

**"We don't negotiate."**

Some companies (notably large tech companies with rigid band structures) genuinely have narrow flex. But you can still ask:
- For a higher band placement within the role
- For a larger signing bonus
- For accelerated first review

> "I respect that. Is it possible I'm being leveled at the right band? Based on my experience with [X, Y, Z], I wonder if [Senior / L5 / etc.] might be a better fit — which would also address the comp gap."

**Creating leverage:**

- Competing offers are the most credible leverage. Get them if you can.
- A clear, evidence-based case for your market value is second best.
- Enthusiasm paired with a specific ask ("I want to say yes today if we can get to $X") signals seriousness without desperation.

**When to walk away:**

Walk away if: the final offer is below your floor *and* non-monetary factors don't compensate; if the company refuses any good-faith negotiation (a signal of future inflexibility); or if you have a better offer and no compelling reason to leave money on the table.

Walking away is not burning a bridge. A polite, gracious decline leaves the door open.

> "After careful consideration, I've decided to go a different direction. I have a lot of respect for the team and hope our paths cross again. Thank you for the time and the offer."

## Raises & Promotions

**Building your case for a raise:**

Document your impact *throughout the year*, not just at review time. Keep a running "brag doc" — a private log of wins, projects shipped, metrics moved, and feedback received.

Structure your case around three pillars:
1. **Impact:** What did you deliver? Quantify wherever possible (revenue, cost savings, time saved, users affected).
2. **Market data:** What does someone with your skills and experience earn in this market? Bring numbers.
3. **Growth:** How have your responsibilities expanded since your last comp adjustment?

**Timing:**

- Ideally, have the conversation **4–6 weeks before** your formal review cycle begins. This is when budgets are being set.
- If you just shipped something big, the week after is a natural moment.
- Never ask right after a miss, a reorg, or during a company-wide hiring freeze — timing affects outcomes.

**The ask:**

> "I'd like to talk about my compensation ahead of the review cycle. I've put together some notes on what I've delivered this year and done some research on where the market is for this role. I'd love to walk through it with you and discuss what a comp adjustment would look like."

Follow up in writing after the conversation. A paper trail protects you and signals seriousness.

**Promotions:**

- Understand the criteria for the next level *before* you're ready for it. Ask your manager: "What does L[X+1] look like, and what would you need to see from me?"
- Then do that work, document it, and make the ask when you can point to evidence.
- If you're told "next cycle," get specifics: *"What milestones between now and then would make this a clear yes?"*
- If you're consistently operating above your level without recognition, name it: *"I've been doing [L+1 responsibilities] for [N months]. I'd like to align my title and comp to reflect that."*
